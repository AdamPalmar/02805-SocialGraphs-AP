'''Janet Coleman''' [[Royal Historical Society|FRHistS]] (born 1945, [[New York City]]<ref>[http://errol.oclc.org/laf/n81-52111.html Library of Congress Name Authority File]</ref>) is a [[United Kingdom|British]] academic and historian of [[political theory]].

She is currently the Professor of Ancient and Medieval Political Thought at the [[London School of Economics]]. She was the first woman to receive a chair in the LSE government department.<ref name="Dispatch">Interview with Janet Coleman, ''Dispatch Box'', no. 4 (March 2008)</ref> Her research interests include ancient Greek and Roman [[political thought]], medieval philosophy, and theories of citizenship and the state.

Coleman studied at [[Sorbonne|L'Ecole Pratique des Hautes Etudes]] in Paris and received her [[Doctor of Philosophy|Ph.D.]] degree from [[Yale University]]. She has held teaching appointments in politics at [[Exeter University]] and on the History Faculty of the [[University of Cambridge]]. 

In 1980 she co-founded (with [[Iain Hampsher-Monk]]) the [[academic journal]] ''[[History of Political Thought (journal)|History of Political Thought]]'', which she continues to co-edit. She is a Fellow of the [[Royal Historical Society]].

Coleman has taught at LSE since 1989, where from 2001 to 2004 she held a  [[Leverhulme Trust|Leverhulme Major Research Fellowship]]. Her lectures in the introductory government course at the LSE are known for her attempts to "'be' political philosophers from the ancient Greeks to Machiavelli."<ref name="Dispatch">Interview with Janet Coleman, ''Dispatch Box'', no. 4 (March 2008)</ref> Coleman plans to retire in 2010.<ref name="Dispatch">Interview with Janet Coleman, ''Dispatch Box'', no. 4 (March 2008)</ref> She has been offered a Global Distinguished Professorship at [[New York University]].

Coleman resides in [[Cambridge]].

'''Paolo da Pergola'''<ref>Paolo della Pergola, Paul of Pergula, Paul of Pergola, Paulus Pergulensis or Pergolensis, Paulus de Pergula</ref> (died 1455, in Venice) was an Italian humanist philosopher, mathematician and [[William of Ockham|Occamist]]<ref>Ennio De Bellis, ''[https://books.google.com/books?id=xgwFAQAAIAAJ&q=Paolo+da+Pergola+vernia&dq= Nicoletto Vernia e Agostino Nifo: aspetti storiografici e metodologici]'', Congedo, 2003, p. 9.</ref> logician. He was a pupil of [[Paul of Venice]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.textmanuscripts.com/home/archives/archivesdescription.php?m%3D107 |title=Archived copy |accessdate=2007-01-28 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20060613041248/http://www.textmanuscripts.com/home/archives/archivesdescription.php?m=107 |archivedate=2006-06-13 |df= }}: ''He became the first publicly paid lecturer in philosophy in Venice, where he was officially honored in a public ceremony. In 1448, he was offered a bishopric, which he refused, and at the end of his life he accepted the administration of the Church of Saint John Almoner.''</ref>

His most important work was probably ''De sensu composito et diviso''.<ref>Printed by 1494; it shares a title with a work of [[William of Heytesbury]].</ref> His logical works were printed early.<ref>''Compendium logicae'' printed by [[Erhard Ratdolt]] in 1481; later in Venice as ''Compendium logicae; De sensu composito et diviso'' (1498); as ''Logica Magistri Pauli Pergolensis''. 1510.[http://edit16.iccu.sbn.it/scripts/iccu_ext.dll?fn=10&i=36208] His ''Dubia'' was printed in 1477.</ref>

He taught at the Scuola di Rialto from 1421 to 1454.<ref>[http://webcache.googleusercontent.com/search?q=cache:vz3olOZn-Z4J:www.unive.it/media/allegato/download/Lettere/Materiale_didattico_Caracciolo/0607_cult_civilta_umanesimo_rinascimento/lineamenti_cult_civ_umanesimo_rinascim-parte2.pdf](PDF), note p.7 (in Italian).</ref> He was teacher and friend of the glassmaker [[Antonio Barovier]].<ref>[http://www.wga.hu/bio_m/b/barovier/biograph.html PDF].</ref>

Among his pupils was also [[Nicoletto Vernia]], a well known professor of philosophy in Padua.<ref>[[Avery Dulles|Avery Robert Dulles]], ''[https://books.google.com/books?id=McYzAAAAMAAJ&q=Paolo+da+Pergola+vernia&dq= Princeps Concordiae: Pico della Mirandola and the scholastic tradition]'', Harvard University Press, 1941, p. 29.</ref>

There is a memorial to him in [[San Giovanni Elemosinario]], Venice.<ref>[http://www.chorusvenezia.org/museo/elemos/2d/giovanni.htm San Giovanni Elemosinario<!-- Bot generated title -->] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20070927145944/http://www.chorusvenezia.org/museo/elemos/2d/giovanni.htm |date=2007-09-27 }}</ref>

'''Duncan Kennedy''' (born 1942) is the Carter Professor of General Jurisprudence at [[Harvard Law School]] and a founder of [[critical legal studies]] as movement and school of thought.

==Education and early career==

Kennedy received an [[Bachelor of Arts|A.B.]] from [[Harvard College]] in 1964 and then worked for two years in the [[CIA]] operation that controlled the [[National Student Association]].<ref>See [http://duncankennedy.net/documents/A%20Semiotics%20of%20Critique.pdf Duncan Kennedy, ''Symposium: Afterword, A Semiotics of Critique,'' 22 Cardozo L. Rev. 1147, 1166-67 (2001).]
</ref>  In 1966 he rejected his "cold war liberalism."<ref>''Id.'' at 1167.</ref>  He quit the CIA<ref>''Id.''</ref> and in 1970 earned an [[Bachelor of Laws|LL.B.]] from [[Yale Law School]]. After completing a clerkship with [[Supreme Court of the United States|Supreme Court]] Justice [[Potter Stewart]], Kennedy joined the Harvard Law School faculty, becoming a full professor in 1976. In March 2010 he received an Honoris Causa (honorary degree) Ph.D. title from the [[University of the Andes (Colombia)|University of the Andes]] in [[Colombia]].

Kennedy has been a member of the [[American Civil Liberties Union]] since 1967. According to his own testimony, he has never forgotten to pay his dues.<ref>Duncan Kennedy, Harvard Law School Lecture, "Globalization of Legal Ideas and Ideology," April 14, 2011.</ref>

==Academic work and influence==
In 1977, together with [[Karl Klare]], [[Mark Kelman]], [[Roberto Mangabeira Unger|Roberto Unger]], and other scholars, Kennedy established the [[critical legal studies|Critical Legal Studies movement]]. Outside legal academia, he is mostly known for his monograph ''[[Legal Education and the Reproduction of Hierarchy]]''*[http://www.duncankennedy.net/documents/Photo%20articles/Legal%20Education%20and%20the%20Reproduction%20of%20Hierarchy_J.%20Leg.%20Ed..pdf], famous for its trenchant critique of American legal education.
